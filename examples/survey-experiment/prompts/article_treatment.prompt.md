# Why some districts are pushing high school start times later

Adolescents' biological sleep cycles run roughly two hours later than
they did in childhood. Through their teenage years they fall asleep
later and need to wake later to get the same sleep. A 7:30 a.m.
school bell forces a teenager out of bed at the equivalent of an
adult's 5:30.

Several large school districts in the United States have shifted high
school start times to 8:30 a.m. or later in recent years.
Peer-reviewed evaluations of those shifts report:

- Roughly 30 minutes more sleep per night, on average, for affected
  students.
- Reduced absenteeism and tardiness in the first period.
- Modest but measurable gains in self-reported attentiveness,
  particularly in the morning hours.
- A small but statistically detectable drop in teen-driver crashes
  during the morning commute.

The American Academy of Pediatrics, the American Medical Association,
and the U.S. Centers for Disease Control have each issued statements
supporting middle and high school start times no earlier than 8:30
a.m. The trade-offs are real — bus routing, after-school activities,
working parents — but the public-health bodies have come down on the
side of later starts.
