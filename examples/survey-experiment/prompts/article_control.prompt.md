# Mechanical clocks: a short history

Mechanical clocks emerged in 13th-century Europe in monastic
communities, where regular hours of prayer required something
sturdier than candle-and-water timekeeping. The earliest devices used
a falling weight, a verge-and-foliot escapement, and a bell that
struck on the hour — quite literally a "clock," from the Latin
*clocca*, meaning bell.

By the late 14th century mechanical clocks were a fixture in town
squares, and by the 17th century the pendulum clock — Christiaan
Huygens, 1656 — improved accuracy from minutes-per-day to
seconds-per-day. The hour, the half hour, and eventually the minute
became *public* units of time, displayed on the same clock face for
the whole community.

The everyday rhythm of work, school, and worship reorganised itself
around the clock. Whatever start time a community settled on, the
mechanism for announcing it had already been around for several
hundred years.
